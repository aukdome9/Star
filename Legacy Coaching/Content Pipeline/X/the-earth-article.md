## Article Body

The tree does not strive to grow deep roots. It simply does — because that is what trees do.

No effort narrative. No morning mantra convincing itself it's a grower of roots. It just grows them, continuously, because depth is what a tree is for. Principle III of Dimensional Reconstructing takes that observation seriously instead of treating it as a nice metaphor: the distance between where you are and where you want to be is almost never a talent gap. It's a systems gap. The tree already runs the system. You're the one still deciding whether to copy it.

### Deep Roots, Measured in the Dark

Roots that only go deep stay brittle. Roots that only go wide stay shallow. The Earth's first move is both at once — mastery in a domain vast and connected enough that it can feed, and be fed by, everything else you know.

There's a real, measured version of this happening underground right now, at night, while nothing appears to move. Forest ecologist Todd Dawson's research on hydraulic redistribution documented deep-rooted trees drawing water from far below the surface and releasing it upward through their own root systems into the dry topsoil — water that shallow-rooted neighbors, sometimes an entirely different species, then draw on to survive the day. In drought conditions, deep roots have been measured contributing several times more to this nighttime water-sharing than shallow roots do. The depth isn't decorative. It's the only reason anything shallow survives the dry season at all.

That's Deep Roots, doing exactly what the doctrine says it does — running deep enough to reach what others can't, and wide enough that the reach doesn't stay private.

### The Wood-Wide Web

The second principle sounds almost too convenient to be literal, until you learn it is. The Symbiotic Network describes an underground web of mutual feeding — the tree circulating resources into a shared system with no guarantee of immediate return, and the system, over time, feeding it back.

In 1997, forest ecologist Suzanne Simard and colleagues published a landmark study in *Nature* using radioactive carbon tracers to follow sugar moving between different tree species connected by a shared network of mycorrhizal fungi. When one tree was artificially shaded and could no longer photosynthesize efficiently, it received a measurable transfer of carbon from a neighboring tree through that fungal network. The finding was significant enough to put the phrase "the wood-wide web" on the cover of *Nature* that August. Trees, it turns out, run the Ten Percent Rule as a documented biological mechanism, not a self-help metaphor — value circulated through a shared web, returning through channels no single tree designed or controls.

The doctrine's claim was never poetic license. It undersold what's actually down there.

### The Seed That Remembers

The third principle is the hardest to trust because it asks you to believe that something enormous can be safely reduced to something tiny without losing anything real. A tree can die completely and, if a single pure seed survives, nothing about the tree's essential code is lost.

Humanity built a literal version of this claim into a mountainside. The Svalbard Global Seed Vault, opened in February 2008 inside a frozen mountain in the Norwegian Arctic, exists for exactly this reason — to hold duplicate seed samples from gene banks around the world as insurance against the loss of any single population, crop, or region. It now safeguards over 1.3 million seed samples, with room for millions more, chosen specifically because permafrost keeps them viable for decades or centuries without power. Nobody built that vault because seeds are convenient. They built it because a seed, correctly kept, is the single most compact, most durable form the full code of a living thing can take.

Micro Mapping asks you to find that seed in your own work — stated in two sentences, still carrying the whole code — before you spend years building something that was never checked against it.
